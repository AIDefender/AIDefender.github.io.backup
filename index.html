Essay for Week4: Literature Review

Equivalence Between PG and SQL

It is shown that there is a precise equivalence between Q-learning and policy gradient methods in the setting of entropy-regularized reinforcement learning. To be accurate, the gradient of the loss function used in n-step Q-learning is equal to the gradient of the loss used in an n-step policy gradient method, including a squared-error term on the value function.

Off-policy Actor-Critic Methods

The notations are slightly different from traditional RL literature. For example, $v_\pi$ for value functions and $\mathbf{P}_\pi[s,s’]$ as transition matrix. In convention, the target policy is $\pi$ and behavior policy is $\mu$.

Prediction Tasks: Learning the Critic

The on-policy Goal is to learn $V$ such that
$$
\sum_{s} d_\pi(s) \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\left[\delta\left(S, A, S^{\prime}\right) | S=s\right]=0
$$
where $\delta\left(S, A, S^{\prime}\right)=R\left(S, A, S^{\prime}\right)+\gamma V\left(S^{\prime}\right)-V(S)$.

In off-policy linear TD, the update $∆_t$ is weighted by $ρ_t$. Gradient TD methods compute the gradient of the projected Bellman error directly. Emphatic TD was proposed since GTD are usually two-time-scale, involving two sets of parameters and two learning rates, which makes it hard to use in practice.

ETD introduces an interest function $i: \mathcal{S} \rightarrow[0, \infty)$ and $M_t=i(S_t)+\gamma_t\rho_{t-1}M_{t-1}$, which is accumulated interests. The update $\Delta_{t}$ is weighted by $\rho_{t} M_{t} .$ In practice, we usually set $i\equiv1$.

Consistent Off-Policy TD weight $\Delta_t$ via $\rho_t\bar{c}(S_t)$, where $\bar{c}(s) \doteq \frac{d_{\pi}(s)}{d_{\mu}(s)}$ is the density ratio. To learn $\bar{c}$ via stochastic approximation, the COP operator was proposed. Even further, $\hat{\gamma}$-discounted COP operator was proposed.

The following are detailed introductions of some articles.

Emphatic TD Learning  TD learning: make online adaptations to the prediction based on one(or several) steps, rather than the whole trajectory. Using IS to reweight the updates of TD in off-policy setting has very high variance. Emphatic weighting is similar to IS in that it also varies emphasis so as to reweight the distribution of linear TD($λ$) updates, but to a different goal. The new goal is to create a weighting equivalent to the followon distribution for the target policy started in the stationary distribution of the behavior policy.

  • stability of on-policy TD(0): many matrix-form annotations; related to positive definite matrix and contraction.
  • instability of off-policy TD(0): distribution mismatch and the $\theta\to2\theta$ problem.

Details of Off-policy TD  The excursion objective is
$$
\sum_{s} d_\mu(s) \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\left[\delta\left(S, A, S^{\prime}\right) | S=s\right]=0
$$
Basic assumption in off-TD is that $\mu(a|s)>0$ for every $s,a$ for which $\pi(a|s)>0$, since it would be impossible to estimate the values under $\pi$ if the actions that $\pi$ would take were never taken by $\mu$ and their consequences were never observed. This is called the assumption of coverage.

The deep reason for the difficulty of off-policy learning is that the behavior policy may take the process to a distribution of states different from that which would be encountered under the target policy, yet the states might appear to be the same or similar because of function approximation.

Stability of Emphatic TD(0)  For comparison, naive off-policy TD update with importance sampling weight $\rho_t$ can be written as
$$
\boldsymbol{\theta}{t+1} \doteq \boldsymbol{\theta}{t}+\rho_{t} \alpha\left(R_{t+1}+\gamma \boldsymbol{\theta}{t}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\phi}{t+1}-\boldsymbol{\theta}{t}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\phi}{t}\right) \boldsymbol{\phi}{t}
$$
where $\boldsymbol{\phi}_t$ are features of the state $t$. The emphatic algorithm introduces the followon trace $F_t$, with $F_0=1$ and
$$
\begin{equation}
F{t} \doteq \gamma \rho_{t-1} F_{t-1}+1, \quad \forall t>0\qquad(1)
\end{equation}
$$
Then the update of emphatic TD(0) is
$$
\boldsymbol{\theta}{t+1} \doteq \boldsymbol{\theta}{t}+\alpha F_{t} \rho_{t}\left(R_{t+1}+\gamma \boldsymbol{\theta}{t}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\phi}{t+1}-\boldsymbol{\theta}{t}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\phi}{t}\right) \boldsymbol{\phi}{t}
$$
In the process of stability reasoning, a diagonal matrix $\mathbf{F}$ is defined with diagonal elements $f(s) \doteq d{\mu}(s) \lim {t \rightarrow \infty} \mathbb{E}{\mu}\left[F_{t} | S_{t}=s\right]$. In general, $\mathbf{f}$ is the expected number of time steps that would be spent in each state during an excursion starting from the behavior distribution $\mathbf{d}μ$, or formally
$$
\mathbf{f}=\mathbf{d}{\mu}+\gamma \mathbf{P}{\pi}^{\top} \mathbf{d}{\mu}+\left(\gamma \mathbf{P}{\pi}^{\top}\right)^{2} \mathbf{d}_{\mu}+\cdots
$$
We regard $(1)$ as its approximation.

Control Tasks: Learning the Actor

The off-policy excursion objective is defined as
$$
J_{\mu} \doteq \sum_{s} d_{\mu}(s) i(s) v_{\pi}(s)
$$
Off-Policy Policy Gradient methods write the gradient of $J_\mu$ as
$$
\nabla J_{\mu}=\sum_{s} d_{\mu}(s) i(s) \sum_{a}\left(q_{\pi}(s, a) \nabla \pi(a | s)+\pi(a | s) \nabla q_{\pi}(s, a)\right)
$$
and ignore the term $\pi(s,a)\nabla q_\pi(s,a)$.

Others proposed an unbiased estimator $Z_t$ of the gradient.
$$
\begin{array}{l}
F_{t}^{(1)} \doteq i\left(S_{t}\right)+\gamma_{t} \rho_{t-1} F_{t-1}^{(1)}, \quad M_{t}^{(1)} \doteq\left(1-\lambda_{1}\right) i\left(S_{t}\right)+\lambda_{1} F_{t}^{(1)} \
Z_{t}^{(1)} \doteq \rho_{t} M_{t}^{(1)} q_{\pi}\left(S_{t}, A_{t}\right) \nabla \log \pi\left(A_{t} | S_{t}\right)
\end{array}
$$
and the ACE algorithm updates $\theta \text { as } \theta_{t+1}=\theta_{t}+\alpha Z_{t}^{(1)}$. The following are detailed introductions of some articles.

Off-PAC with Emphatic Weights 

This article gives an approach to compute the gradient of $J_\mu(\mathbf{\theta})$.
Theorem(Off-policy Policy Gradient Theorem):
  
$$
\frac{\partial J_{\mu}(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}}=\sum_{s} m(s) \sum_{a} \frac{\partial \pi(s, a ; \boldsymbol{\theta})}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}} q_{\pi}(s, a)
$$
where $m:S\to[0,\infty)$ is the emphatic weighting, defined as
$$
\mathbf{m}^{\top} \stackrel{\text { def }}{=} \mathbf{i}^{\top}\left(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{P}{\pi, \gamma}\right)^{-1}
$$
where the vector $\mathbf{i} \in \mathbb{R}^{|S|}$ has entries $\mathbf{i}(s) \stackrel{\text { def }}{=} d{\mu}(s) i(s)$ and $\mathbf{P}{\pi, \gamma} \in \mathbb{R}^{|s|\times|s|}$ is the matrix with entries $\mathbf{P}{\pi, \gamma}\left(s, s^{\prime}\right) \stackrel{\text { det }}{=} \sum_{a} \pi(s, a ; \boldsymbol{\theta}) \mathrm{P}\left(s, a, s^{\prime}\right) \gamma\left(s, a, s^{\prime}\right)$.

The proof of the theorem involves a matrix representation and a fixed-point style equation.
